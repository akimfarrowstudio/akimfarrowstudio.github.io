FIELD NOTES / PROCESS WRITING

The genesis of this work began with the geometric silhouettes of the Great Zimbabwe ruins. I wanted to translate the dry-stone masonry of the ancestors into the industrial language of the present—raw, heavy steel. The challenge was maintaining the 'breath' of the stone walls while using rigid, plate material.

I spent three weeks in the Masvingo province, sketching the way light hits the conical tower at dawn. That specific angle of incidence informed the primary structural lean of the sculpture. The steel was left to oxidize in the open air of the foundry for six months to achieve a patina that mimics the lichen-covered granite of the original site.

The interior of the sculpture is hollow, acting as a resonant chamber for the wind. When the air moves through the Chicago lakeside gallery where it was first shown, it produces a low-frequency hum—a literal echo across geography and time.
